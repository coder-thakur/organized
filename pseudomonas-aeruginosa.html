Pseudomonas aeruginosa

A multidrug-resistant Gram-negative bacterium associated with severe healthcare-associated infections and biofilm formation

Overview

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is an opportunistic pathogen that frequently causes urinary tract infections in hospitalized and immunocompromised patients. Its ability to form biofilms and resistance to multiple antibiotics make it a challenging pathogen.

Molecular Markers

  • Exopolysaccharide production (alginate) facilitating biofilm formation
  • Toxins such as exotoxin A contributing to virulence
  • Quorum sensing systems regulating gene expression and biofilm development
  • Efflux pumps conferring multidrug resistance

Immune Response

The host immune response includes neutrophilic infiltration and cytokine production. However, biofilm formation and alginate production impair immune cell access, promoting persistent infections in immunocompromised individuals.

Epidemiology

  • Commonly associated with catheter-related UTIs in hospital settings
  • Highly resistant to multiple antibiotics
  • Significant cause of morbidity in immunocompromised patients
